FAQs

How often do you make tea by simply leaving the kettle to boil, or by removing the tea bag only once you remember that it’s there?
If so, you have been unknowingly – and significantly – altering the flavour of the brew, more often than not drinking little more than burnt leaves.
Read on for a guide on the perfect cuppa…

Black If you’ve been buying teabags prior to now, black is likely what you’re most familiar with. Such teabags usually consist of a low grade blend – multi-origin, created with the leftover particles of higher grade (larger leaf) products. In China black tea is referred to as “red tea”. Loose leaf black tea has a much stronger, full-bodied taste
Green Well-known for its health benefits, green tea from a teabag doesn’t hold a candle to the real thing – the brew should not be bitter, the most common cause of which occurring through burning the leaves (through using water that’s too hot). At the correct temperature, green tea is often lightly grassy - although the roasted hojicha could fool you into thinking you're drinking something entirely different!
White A much lighter tasting profile than other types, white tea is more labour-intensive to harvest and therefore more expensive than most other teas.
Herbal AKA tisanes. The debate rages on as to whether this truly qualifies as tea as the leaves are picked from an entirely different type of plant. These teas usually have one overriding flavour - often floral (like chamomile, hibiscus) or fruit-infused (berries and apples are most common).

Whilst the exact time is dependent on the specific tea, some general guidelines are…
Black To create the strong, full-bodied flavour that black teas are renowned for, steep for up to five minutes.
Green Too long a steeping time can impart bitterness, too short and the tea will lack any real flavour. Between two and three minutes is a happy medium.

Good Starter Teas
If you're just stepping away from teabags into the world of loose leaf the selection can be overwhelming. How can you possibly know the meaning behind OP, FOP, GFOP, TGFOP, FTGFOP ... SFTGFOP? Rather than trying to decipher the increasingly long acronyms (they all refer to leaf quality, by the way - the latter, if you're interested, stands for Superior Finest Tippy Golden Flowery Orange Pekoe. Really rolls off the tongue) we've put together a selection of starter teas to get you on your way. We recommend you skip the milk and sugar, to get nature's full flavour:
Black Darjeeling - black teas are well-known to carry more caffeine than other types, so if you're moving over from coffee Darjeeling's a good place to start. It has the expected strength, with a subtle underlying sweetness.
Green Sencha - Sencha is widely available, and quintessentially green. Unflavoured (unlike the more common Green Jasmine) and extremely grassy (in a good way!), Sencha's the most popular brew in Japan.

Bad Starter Teas
And with the good must come the bad. We're not saying these are bad teas (does such a thing exist?) but for beginners you can easily create poor brews - and spend a lot whilst doing so - on the following:
Pu’erh - more often sold in (expensive!) "cakes" that need to be finely chipped away at, with brew times ranging from 30 seconds to several minutes. You really need to know what you're doing.
Matcha - Matcha is a consumed in powder form, not leaf. Whislt no strainer is needed here, a good brewing technique is essential - and such technique requires tools in the form of a very small bowl and a very large whisk. Without the latter you'll likely end up with a very lumpy brew. And did we mention the cost?
Oolong - less tricky then the above two, but Oolong manages to be a very broad umbrella for a multitude of teas. Whilst it's easy to see the similarities between other varities, oolongs vary hugely - finding one that you like can be a very time-consuming (tea-consuming?) process.
